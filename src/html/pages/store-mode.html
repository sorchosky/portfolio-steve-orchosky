Store Mode

Helping shoppers navigate their store and increasing average basket size by 37%

Overview

Target’s digital in-store shopping companion

As Senior UX Designer, I helped deliver Target’s Store Mode MVP, a mobile-first in-store digital experience focused on utility and navigation. The goal was to empower shoppers to easily find products, get help, and navigate the store using their mobile device, bridging the gap between physical and digital shopping.

Six months after ramping traffic to 100% of shoppers, we’ve observed that on average 62% of shoppers reused Store Mode within 7 days of prior use and average order value increased by 37% when Store Mode was used during shopping.

Context

Background

###

Target observed that 15% of app reviews referenced a desire for contextually-aware in-store features. Although guests frequently turned to the app while shopping, core in-store functionality was limited. The Store Mode MVP aimed to solve this problem by creating a seamless, intuitive experience that enhanced store visits while driving key business outcomes like repeat usage and increased basket size.

Design Process

###

Initial shopper insights were gathered during a company hackathon, where guests provided direct feedback on interactive prototypes. Of several concepts tested, guests gravitated toward a map-based navigation tool to help locate products in-store. This insight informed the foundation of the three-layer framework:

  • Landing Page — Entry point into the store experience
  • Listing Page — A structured list of search results or categories
  • Locate Page — A map view showing item locations in-store

The framework leveraged mental models from native map platforms (Apple Maps, Google Maps) to ensure familiarity and ease of use. My role was to evolve early designs into a scalable, utility-driven experience, balancing intuitive interactions with moments of product discovery.

Key Design Decision

###

One major challenge was deciding what, if anything, to surface on the initial landing map. I advocated for launching with a blank map, opting not to assume shopper intent with preloaded content like deals or shopping list items (which had historically low engagement).

While this non-intrusive approach respected user autonomy, post-launch research revealed that shoppers desired more guidance on first use—a valuable learning for future iterations.

Cross-Functional Collaboration

###

I worked with stakeholders to ensure our design decisions aligned with technical constraints, while still prioritizing the guest experience. I created spaces for regular visibility of design status and decisions through hosting regular engineering discussions, as well as facilitating multiple cross-organizational design leader critiques.

Trade-offs were made transparently and collaboratively to balance consistent progress and core guest utility.

Impact

###

The MVP saw immediate traction:

  • 62% of shoppers reused Store Mode within 7 days of initial use
  • Basket sizes increased by 37% when Store Mode was used during shopping

Beyond quantitative metrics, our research team conducted post-launch shop-alongs and usability tests. These revealed that Store Mode's entry point was not as visible as intended, and that bolder, more interruptive design treatments may be needed for future discovery.

Next Steps

###

Following a successful MVP launch, we identified three key areas for iteration:

  1. Improve discovery and visibility of Store Mode entry points
  2. Address out-of-stock scenarios with intelligent alternatives
  3. Explore new product assortments that foster discovery while shopping in store

The MVP proved that context-aware design can deepen engagement and boost purchase behavior. With a solid foundation in place, future work will focus on evolving the experience into a core part of Target's omnichannel ecosystem.
